1. Navigation (Movement Mode)

When you first start the app, you are in Movement Mode. Your selection is highlighted in Dark Blue.

    Arrow Keys: Move the selection cursor around the grid.
    Scrolling: If you move past the edge of the visible window (e.g., moving to row 21), the grid will automatically scroll to keep your cursor in view.
    Enter: Switches to Editing Mode for the selected cell.


2. Editing (Input Mode)

When you press Enter, the status bar highlight turns Dark Green.

    Typing: Anything you type appears in the status bar at the bottom.
    Backspace: Deletes the last character typed.
    Enter: Saves the content to the cell and triggers a Global Recalculation (updating all formulas).
    Escape: Discards your current typing and returns to Movement Mode without saving.


3. Data Entry Rules

The spreadsheet handles three types of data:

Labels (Text Data)
Labels are descriptive headers like "Total," "Price," or "Date." They are displayed exactly as you type them. Their primary purpose is to provide context for the numbers around them; they are not used in mathematical calculations.

Numbers (Values)
Numbers, sureated as raw numeric values. Unlike labels, the software recognizes these as quantities that can be added, subtracted, or used in complex functions.

Formulas (Calculations)
Formulas are the "engine" of the spreadsheet. An example would be =A1+B1. These allow the spreadsheet to perform tasks automatically and update the results if the referenced numbers change.

The engine uses standard "A1" notation:

    References: Column letter + Row number (e.g., C10).
    Math: = (A1 + A2) * 1.15 (calculates the sum of A1 and A2 plus a 15% tax).
    Error Handling: If you reference an empty cell, it treats it as 0. If you make a math error (like dividing by zero), the cell will display #ERR.


4. Technical Architecture

Under the hood, the code is split into three main logic loops:

    The Grid (Data Store): A 2D array of Cell objects. Each object stores the "hidden" formula and the "visible" display result.
    The Parser (The Brain): When a formula is calculated, it uses a Regex (Regular Expression) to find cell coordinates, look up their values, and then hands the final math string to DataTable.Compute to solve the equation.
    The Renderer (The View): A simple loop that draws the headers, then loops through a "window" of cells (the offR and offC variables) to draw only what fits on your screen.


💡 Quick Advice

    Column Limits: This simplified version supports columns A through Z.
    Recalculation: To keep things fast, the app recalculates the entire sheet every time you press Enter on a cell. In a tiny clone like this, it's nearly instantaneous.
    Truncation: If a word or number is longer than 10 characters, the display will cut it off to keep the columns aligned.
    You need to increase your terminal's resolution a bit (99x as from Linux), because at very low resolutions the image may appear glitched.
